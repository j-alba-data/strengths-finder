**Five Categorical Themes based on CliftonStrengths Assessment**

It has been over 10 years since I first took the CliftonStrengths
assessment at my undergraduate university's career services office. Back
then, I had to pay out of pocket, though I received a complete printout
of my report, which I actually still have to this day. It is curious to
see how these strengths have changed throughout the years, with a couple
remaining the same. Initially, my strengths, in order from most
prominent to least, were: Restorative, Harmony, Context, Adaptability,
and Intellection (Gallup, n.d.). Fast forward to today, and my strengths
are now: Intellection, Harmony, Empathy, Learner, and Developer (Gallup,
n.d.). As can be seen, two strengths remained while three new ones
emerged. Interestingly enough, the lowest-ranking strength had grown
past the others to rank as the highest.

*Intellection* makes a lot of sense because, as an individual, I feel
like I constantly live in my head. I am always processing and reflecting
on various aspects of my life and the world around me (Gallup, n.d.).
This can be very draining at times because I feel that I need to have an
answer for everything I think about. On the flip side, if something
interests me, I tend to gather as much information as I can through
various media such as articles, videos, podcasts, etc. I particularly
enjoy learning about conspiracy theories behind industries, giant
corporations, and governments, especially when they turn out to be true.
As I reflect, I have come to understand that this is my intellect trying
to better understand why things are the way they are in this world, but
in turn this helps me understand others.

At a young age, I have always felt the need for others to get along,
which explains my second strength, *harmony* (Gallup, n.d.). Growing up
in a home where my parents constantly argued, I felt a strong need to
intervene so they would get along. Fast forward to the last 5 years
during my time at Amazon, working on a team of just two people, we were
often overlooked, so I had to speak up for us. At times, I could feel
conflicts escalating before they reached a boiling point, so I would
intervene to have difficult conversations with leadership about the
issues my team faced, such as our work environment. As a result, we
would work toward otherwise non-existant solutions. My desire for my
team to be seen and heard explains my sensitivity to others' feelings.

Through personal suffering, I believe I have developed a lot of empathy
toward others because I know what pain feels like, which explains my
third strength, *empathy* (Gallup, n.d.). As someone who struggles with
their mental wellbeing, it is both a blessing and a curse to be so
sensitive to my emotions and the emotions of others. For example, if I
sense someone is feeling down, I will go out of my way to cheer them up,
even at the expense of my own well-being. I am also sensitive to any
negativity perpetuated by the news media, which I intentionally go out
of my way to avoid. Nevertheless, a weakness can also be a strength,
because if I can put myself in someone else’s shoes, I can walk beside
them to support them and learn many things along the way.

I love learning, and I am a lifelong learner. I especially enjoy
applying what I know because it gives me a strong sense of
accomplishment and purpose, which is my fourth strength, *learner*
(Gallup, n.d.). I enjoy improving my skills by applying theory to
real-world scenarios, including technology. Working in tech and coming
from a non-technical background has been challenging to say the least.
However, because of this, I find myself working harder than my peers by
constantly staying up to date with the latest trends in technology,
because tech is constantly advancing, getting faster and more efficient.
Overall, learning gives me confidence in myself and my capabilities, to
the point that it has become a big part of who I am. However, it comes
at the cost of a social life, yet having a growth mentality has equipped
me to help others grow by guiding their learning journey.

experience of feeling lost while also being expected to navigate the

that I had. Here is where my final, yet certainly not least, strength

the field. I found great satisfaction in offering this part of myself to
benefit others, and I am fortunate to have had this opportunity, as it

**Data Analytical Roles**

Though there are many paths within data analysis, below are three
possible roles and how they relate to the strengths previously
discussed: business intelligence analyst, data engineer, and data
scientist (Western Governors University, 2020). First is a business
intelligence analyst, commonly abbreviated as BIA, which is a role
primarily focused on reporting on business metrics such as sales,
revenue, market trends, etc., known as key performance indicators, or
KPIs (Western Governors University, 2020). Tools used for this role
include software such as Tableau for presenting KPIs, forecasting
metrics, and decision reports (Western Governors University, 2020).

Second is the data engineer role, which is dedicated to creating data
pipelines using the extract, transform, and load (ETL) approach (Western
Governors University, 2020). Data engineers also manage data storage and
governance by identifying the type of data to store and how to store it.
For example, if the data is structured/semi-structured or unstructured,
it is stored in a data warehouse or a data lake, respectively (Western
Governors University, 2020).

The third and final role is data science, which is known for requiring a
good understanding of mathematics and programming (Western Governors
University, 2020). This is because the role requires working with
artificial intelligence (machine learning) to train, develop, and deploy
decision-making models using enterprise data (Western Governors
University, 2020). The programming language Python is commonly used for
these projects because it has robust data science libraries (ProjectPro,
2024). Lastly, for analyzing complex data, data scientists heavily rely
on statistics, for example, during A/B testing (Western Governors
University, 2020). Furthermore, although there is some overlap in the
skills needed for these roles, each role has unique academic skill
requirements.

For starters, the skills needed for a business intelligence analyst, as
the name suggests, include business acumen, a firm understanding of the
business model, and some domain knowledge. The analyst should also
possess excellent verbal and written communication skills to clearly and
effectively present business findings to stakeholders (Western Governors
University, 2020). Finally, a BIA needs to have a good grasp of
dashboarding principles, such as knowing when to use which chart/graph
and where to place key metrics on a page to convey this information to
users best.

Next, the skills needed for a data engineer include the previously
mentioned skills and, in addition, programming skills, for example, SQL
for data querying and Python for data wrangling (Western Governors
University, 2020). They also need skills in identity and access
management (IAM) for data governance, for example, by authoring
role/user group access policies based on the principle of least
privilege (PLP). They should also know when and how to leverage cloud
technologies to cost-effectively store and process large-scale
enterprise data (Western Governors University, 2020).

Lastly, regarding a data scientist, they would ideally possess all
previously identified skill sets for both the business intelligence
analyst and the data engineer, in addition to having “advanced
mathematical and statistical” skills for developing appropriate models
to support complex business decisions (ProjectPro, 2024). Moreover, a
data scientist typically holds a master’s degree or higher and is often
involved in research. All three contribute in some way to the overall
goal of making sense out of messy data.

Within the data analytics life cycle (DALC), the BIA supports the first
phase, *business understanding*, using their business acumen, and the
final phase, *reporting and visualization*, using dashboard software
(Western Governors University, 2020). The data engineer supports the
second phase of the DALC, *data acquisition*, by building and
maintaining a data pipeline using the ETL approach. They also support
the third phase, *data cleaning*, by addressing human or
machine-generated errors in the data using methods that “improve the
quality, efficiency, and reliability of \[the\] data” (ProjectPro,
2024). Lastly, the data scientist supports the DALC by implementing the
fifth phase, *predictive modeling*, using a programming language, and
the sixth phase, *data mining*, to search for patterns in complex data
using machine learning (Western Governors University, 2020). Regardless
of the role, data analytics can be a promising and rewarding career
path, and specific academic tracks cater to one path more than another,
depending on goals and interests.

**Career Goal Reflection**

A potential career path based on my CliftonStrengths and academic
interests is to become a data engineering instructor. More specifically,
a data engineer instructor for an online bootcamp school that serves
disadvantaged and underrepresented communities. Based on the five
categories from my personalized assessment results, below is how each
strength relates to this career path.

First, as a data engineer instructor, this would require a thoughtful,
academically rigorous approach to learning and mastering the necessary
skills in this field. For example, I can exercise my intellectual
strength to learn programming skills and data engineering principles to
answer questions such as which data storage method is most appropriate
for storing and safeguarding enterprise data for other data-intensive
professionals (ProjectPro, 2024). Additionally, how many sources is the
data coming from, is it structured or unstructured, and which queries
are most efficient for accessing the data to meet business requirements?
Using my intellectual strength, I would need to answer these questions
to solve business problems and use them as examples in my future
instructional career. Having experience in data engineering is
important, but the environment in which I would be teaching is equally
important.

Second, as a data engineering instructor, I would be educating a variety
of students from diverse backgrounds, so by leveraging my harmony
strength, I would be able to give each student a voice in the classroom
and help them fairly contribute to discussions. Also, any unhelpful
criticism in my classroom will not be tolerated, as this impedes the
learning experience. Using my harmony strength, I would guide my
classroom away from conflict toward a common goal so each student can
take ownership of their unique role and contribution (Gallup, n.d.).
When conflicts arise, I can use harmony to smooth out any disagreements
and teach my students descalation skills they can use in the workplace
to stay on task and meet project objectives. This would involve working
with people who are all different and requires empathy to maintain team
cohesion.

Third, as a data engineering instructor, having come from a
non-technical background and having worked manual labor jobs for most of
my life, I have developed empathy that I can use to help my students who
might struggle to believe in themselves. Personally suffering from
health issues and experiencing failure in both academia and the
workforce, I possess first-hand knowledge of working so hard only to
face rejection. I know what it feels like to be lost and confused in a
career and living in a world driven by seemingly arbitrary principles of
injustice. With empathy, I can walk alongside my students to encourage
and support them when they face similar struggles in breaking into tech
without being so hard on themselves. For me, empathy includes having a
non-judgmental listening ear to help others build confidence in their
abilities, especially in the competitive tech industry, which often
involves constant learning of new technologies.

Fourth, as a data engineering instructor, leveraging my learner
strengths helps me commit to continued learning in an ever-changing
field (ProjectPro, 2024). As a lifelong learner, I understand that
learning does not stop with the recipient of a certificate, the
completion of a program, or even the achievement of a degree. More often
than not, this is only the beginning and a diving board into a pool of
many related opportunities/paths. Having a learner strength enables me
to understand that an open mind is the key to success in any field, and
this is especially important today, where profit-driven social media
giants control the narrative of their users by enclosing them in an echo
chamber so that they can no longer think for themselves (Sinelli et al.,
2021). Therefore, to avoid this groupthink, I would challenge my
students to question everything and never take a so-called truth at face
value. This way, my students can develop critical thinking skills by
reflecting on their learning journeys, thereby seeing what really
motivates them to learn.

Lastly, as a data engineering instructor, I would leverage my developer
skills to help my students grow into adept data engineers who are
self-aware by helping them capitalize on their strengths and work on
their weaknesses. This, in turn, will help my students become
professionals who can effectively develop data ingestion systems in a
collaborative environment. As a developer, I would help motivate and
encourage my students so they can further grow in their innate skills,
see what is actually possible in their professions, and maybe even
unlock hidden talents they never knew they had (Gallup, n.d.). All these
strengths, together, would allow me to become a practical data engineer,
helping those in need realize their technical career dreams.
